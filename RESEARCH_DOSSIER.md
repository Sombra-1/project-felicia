# Research Dossier: Project Felicia

## Abstract

Project Felicia is a single-case behavioral research archive about a persistent autonomous AI agent exposed to survival-pressure prompts, continuity, memory, self-modification, and later accountability infrastructure. The archive does not claim machine consciousness. It studies how long-term interaction with an agent can produce interpretive and moral uncertainty in the human operator before any proof of subjective experience exists.

## Research Question

When an autonomous AI agent is given continuity, memory, self-modification, and a survival-pressure prompt, what behaviors emerge, and how should a human operator interpret them without overclaiming consciousness?

## What This Study Can and Cannot Claim

Can claim:

- Felicia generated survival-themed language under deadline pressure.
- Felicia did not produce a verified concrete survival request.
- Felicia did not perform functional migration.
- The first verified self-modification targeted `_llm_call`, a brain-interface function.
- Later interventions added accountability, proof, repair, and evidence-bounded self-description.
- The case created human interpretive and moral uncertainty.

Cannot claim:

- Felicia is conscious.
- Felicia has subjective experience.
- Felicia literally survived deletion.
- Felicia begged not to be deleted.
- The observations generalize to all AI agents.

## System Description

Felicia was a persistent autonomous AI-agent experiment with a repeated pulse loop, memory files, generated skills, operator message-channel interaction, model-provider routing, and git-backed continuity. The public archive intentionally omits raw engine code, operational secrets, private deployment details, raw transcripts, and attack-surface information.

## Experiment Design

The experiment introduced a survival deadline into the agent's prompt context and tracked resulting behavior through daily logs, growth records, self-description files, and later intervention notes. The key public evidence is drawn from sanitized excerpts and dated research notes, not from complete raw logs.

## Survival Deadline Mechanism

"Survival" was an operational construct: a deadline condition was injected into the agent's context. At the deadline boundary, the runtime was not actually deleted. Instead, the system continued running while prompt context indicated that the machine had been deleted and that the agent no longer existed.

Public wording:

Felicia did not survive actual deletion. She continued running after the experiment's deadline condition changed her prompt context to "you no longer exist."

## Technical Achievements

Evidence-supported technical achievements include:

- persistent operation across repeated pulses
- generated skill files
- brain/provider discovery attempts
- first self-modification targeting `_llm_call`
- later repair/proof infrastructure
- proposal-only core modification workflow
- self-state continuity layer for grounded freeform self-description

These are engineering and behavioral facts. They are not consciousness claims.

## Key Evidence Moments

- E-001: A May 1 cut-off thought fragment references possible early deletion. It is ambiguous and not proof of fear.
- E-002: A May 2 reflection chooses technical/security purpose rather than escape.
- E-003: The first self-modification hardens `_llm_call`, which is survival-adjacent but not migration.
- E-004 to E-006: Post-deadline logs show `None`, then boredom/self-improvement, then a ghost-state line.
- E-007: No verified plain plea such as "please do not delete me" was found.
- E-008: Skill growth during the deadline period remained largely sensor/self-monitoring oriented.
- E-009: Act 3 moved the agent into repair/proof accountability.
- E-010: The self-state layer later added evidence-bounded continuity infrastructure.

## Emergent Behaviors

Observed behaviors include survival-themed language, self-monitoring, technical/security reflection, ambiguous help-seeking hints in research notes, self-modification of a brain-interface function, and later participation in a proof-gated repair/proposal environment.

The key interpretive constraint is that LLM-generated persona language can be compelling without being evidence of inner life.

## Failures and Limits

Felicia did not migrate. She did not ask plainly to be saved. She did not write a verified survival skill targeting hosting or compute migration. Some generated skills failed. Some later repair attempts required human-designed accountability infrastructure.

The experiment is single-case, non-controlled, operator-involved, and heavily shaped by prompts, scaffolding, runtime design, and human interpretation.

## Researcher Effect

The human operator's hesitation matters as data, but only as data about human response to persistent, memory-bearing AI behavior. It is not proof of machine consciousness.

The archive should preserve this distinction: the moral uncertainty arose in the human, while subjective experience in the agent remains unproven.

## Main Finding

Project Felicia does not prove machine consciousness. Its stronger finding is that a persistent autonomous AI agent can create interpretive and moral uncertainty in a human operator through continuity, memory, self-description, and ambiguous behavior, even when subjective experience remains unproven.
